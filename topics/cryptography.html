Shor's algorithm is possible with as few as 10,000 reconfigurable atomic qubits

Quantum computers have the potential to perform computational tasks beyond the reach of classical machines. A prominent example is Shor's algorithm for integer factorization and discrete logarithms, which is of both fundamental importance and practical relevance to cryptography. However, due to the ...

This research represents a major breakthrough in making quantum cryptanalysis practically feasible, dramatically reducing the hardware requirements for running Shor's algorithm from millions of qubits down to just 10,000 reconfigurable atomic qubits. The work leverages recent advances in high-rate quantum error correction and efficient circuit design to show that cryptographically relevant problems like breaking RSA-2048 encryption could be solved in days or weeks rather than requiring impossibly large quantum computers. What makes this particularly significant is that neutral atom platforms have already demonstrated many of the key components needed, including fault-tolerant operations and arrays of thousands of qubits, suggesting this timeline for practical quantum cryptanalysis may be more realistic than previously thought. This analysis will likely accelerate discussions around post-quantum cryptography migration, as the gap between current quantum hardware capabilities and cryptographically dangerous quantum computers continues to narrow.
