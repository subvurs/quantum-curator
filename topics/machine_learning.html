Quantum Convolutional Neural Networks for Groundwater Heat Plume Prediction: A Surrogate Modeling Approach

Quantum machine learning methods are increasingly explored for modeling complex environmental systems, including groundwater heat plume dynamics. In this work, we explore a Quantum Convolutional Neural Network (QCNN) as a surrogate model for predicting temperature variations in groundwater induced b...

This article demonstrates one of the first attempts to deploy a quantum convolutional neural network as a surrogate model for a real‑world environmental problem, showing that QCNNs can be trained to predict groundwater heat plume dynamics even on noisy intermediate‑scale devices. By compressing high‑dimensional simulation data into a few representative parameters and testing across ideal simulators, noisy backends and IBM’s 127‑qubit Kyiv processor with error‑mitigation, the work provides a concrete benchmark of how near‑term quantum hardware handles complex machine‑learning pipelines. Although classical neural nets still outperform the quantum version in accuracy, the study highlights a viable pathway for hybrid quantum‑classical modeling that could eventually accelerate large‑scale environmental simulations and sensor‑fusion tasks as hardware improves.
