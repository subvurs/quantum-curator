Entanglement in quantum channel discrimination: sometimes less is more

Entanglement is known to be a powerful resource that improves performance in various quantum information and computational tasks. A standard example of such a phenomenon is the possibility of perfectly discriminating all four Pauli operations in a single shot via the superdense coding protocol. Whil...

This article challenges a fundamental assumption in quantum information theory by demonstrating that entanglement isn't always beneficial for quantum channel discrimination tasks. The researchers present concrete examples where maximally entangled states perform no better than random guessing, while separable (non-entangled) states can achieve perfect discrimination between certain quantum channels. This counterintuitive finding has significant implications for quantum sensing and metrology applications, where practitioners typically assume that more entanglement leads to better performance. The work provides a systematic framework for identifying when entanglement helps versus hurts, offering practical guidance for optimizing quantum protocols in sensing and communication systems.
