Hunting for quantum advantage in electronic structure calculations is a highly non-trivial task

In light of major developments over the past decades in both quantum computing and simulations on classical hardware, it is a serious challenge to identify a real-world problem where quantum advantage is expected to appear. In quantum chemistry, electronic structure calculations of strongly correlat...

This work delivers a reality check for quantum computing's promised advantage in chemistry simulations by demonstrating that classical algorithms can tackle previously "quantum-only" problems with remarkable efficiency. The researchers successfully computed electronic structures for complex iron-sulfur clusters that were recently added to IBM and RIKEN's Quantum Advantage Tracker, using advanced density matrix renormalization group methods to handle systems with nearly 90 electrons across over 100 orbitals. This benchmark establishes a new high bar for classical performance in strongly correlated quantum chemistry problems, forcing the quantum computing community to look beyond current target molecules when searching for genuine quantum advantage. The results underscore a crucial point often overlooked in the quantum hype: as classical algorithms continue advancing rapidly, the moving goalposts for quantum supremacy make identifying practical quantum advantage increasingly challenging.
