Learning and Generating Mixed States Prepared by Shallow Channel Circuits

Learning quantum states from measurement data is a central problem in quantum information and computational complexity. In this work, we study the problem of learning to generate mixed states on a finite-dimensional lattice. Motivated by recent developments in mixed state phases of matter, we focus ...

This work tackles one of quantum computing's fundamental challenges: how to efficiently learn and recreate unknown quantum states from limited measurement data, which is crucial for everything from quantum sensing to state verification. The researchers prove that mixed quantum states prepared by shallow circuits can be learned and reproduced using only polynomial resources, even without knowing the original preparation method—a significant theoretical advance that could make quantum state learning practically feasible. What makes this particularly exciting is the connection to quantum generative models, suggesting these techniques could enable new approaches for generating complex quantum states efficiently, while also inspiring improvements to classical machine learning methods like diffusion models. The focus on "trivial phase" states provides a concrete framework for understanding which quantum states can be efficiently learned, offering both theoretical insights and potential practical algorithms for near-term quantum devices.
