Mechanical inputs boost diamond quantum sensor states as Q factor tops one million

Most people think of diamonds as high-end adornments. Not Ania Bleszynski Jayich. The UC Santa Barbara physicist sees diamonds, which she grows in the UC Quantum Foundry, as a potentially powerful foundation for quantum sensors. Sensors are currently much farther along in their development than othe...

This research highlights how quantum sensing applications are racing ahead of quantum computing by leveraging diamond's exceptional properties for practical devices that work with far fewer qubits. While quantum computers struggle with the massive overhead of error correction requiring hundreds of thousands of qubits, diamond quantum sensors can deliver real-world functionality with minimal quantum resources, making them among the most commercially viable quantum technologies today. The achievement of a Q factor exceeding one million demonstrates remarkable coherence in these diamond-based systems, potentially enabling ultra-precise measurements for applications ranging from medical imaging to geological surveying. This work underscores why quantum sensing may be the first quantum technology to achieve widespread commercial deployment, paving the way for broader quantum adoption.
