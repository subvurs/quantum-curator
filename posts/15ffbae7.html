A counterexample to the strong spin alignment conjecture

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 26, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article delivers a significant blow to a fundamental conjecture in quantum information theory by providing the first concrete counterexample to the strong spin alignment conjecture, which has important implications for understanding quantum channel capacities and information transmission. The counterexample cleverly exploits the subtle quantum mechanical principle that certain two-qubit states cannot be embedded consistently within a larger three-qubit system, revealing deep constraints on how quantum correlations can be globally realized. While this result might seem purely theoretical, it actually impacts our understanding of quantum error correction and channel coding limits, areas crucial for building reliable quantum computers. The work also opens new research directions by proposing a refined "compatibility-constrained" version of the conjecture that could better capture the true behavior of these quantum systems.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

The spin alignment conjecture was originally formulated in connection with the additivity of coherent information for a class of quantum channels known as platypus channels. Recently, a stronger majorization-based version was proposed by M. A. Alhejji and E. Knill [Commun. Math. Phys. 405, 119, 2024], asserting that the spectrum of the alignment operator is always majorized by that of the perfectly aligned configuration. In this letter, we show that this strong spin alignment conjecture is false in general by constructing an explicit counterexample in the smallest unresolved case, namely three qubits. The example uses two-body states that are not jointly compatible with any single three-qubit global state, which naturally leads to a compatibility-constrained variant of the conjecture.
