An Entanglement Measure from Quantum Optimal Transport

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics June 03, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article introduces a groundbreaking approach to measuring quantum entanglement by borrowing concepts from optimal transport theory, a mathematical framework traditionally used in economics and logistics. The proposed measure elegantly satisfies all the fundamental requirements for quantifying entanglement within a single geometric framework, potentially offering a more unified and computationally tractable way to characterize this essential quantum resource. What makes this particularly exciting is the direct connection to experimental entanglement detection through entanglement witnesses, providing a bridge between abstract theoretical measures and practical quantum state verification. This work could lead to more efficient algorithms for entanglement quantification and better methods for validating entangled states in quantum devices, making it valuable for both fundamental research and near-term quantum applications.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

We propose a bipartite entanglement measure $E(ρ)$ defined as the minimal order-1 quantum Wasserstein distance from a state to the set of separable states. Owing to the universal data-processing inequality of the Wasserstein metric, the measure satisfies all fundamental axioms within a single geometric framework. A Lipschitz dual formulation yields explicit lower bounds for pure and mixed states, a sharp constant for two-qubit systems, and an expected value for Haar-random pure states. We further establish a quantitative connection to entanglement witnesses: any negative witness expectation value certifies a lower bound on $E$, and the dual variational bound is exactly the maximal violation achievable by a Lipschitz-1 witness. The approach naturally provides subadditivity, trace-distance estimates, and bounds on local observables, while pointing toward large-deviation conjectures. This work furnishes a versatile paradigm at the interface of entanglement theory, optimal transport, and experimental entanglement detection.
