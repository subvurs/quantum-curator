Quantum resonance encryption for secure data storage and communication with quantum kicked top

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics June 01, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article presents a fascinating approach to quantum data protection using quantum kicked tops - essentially spinning quantum systems that can create complex, chaotic dynamics when operated at specific resonance conditions. The protocol addresses a critical real-world problem: how to keep your data private even from the quantum cloud service provider itself, which becomes increasingly important as quantum computing moves toward shared, cloud-based access models. What makes this particularly clever is that authorized users can perfectly recover their data while any unauthorized access attempts only see scrambled, mixed quantum states that reveal nothing useful. While the full protocol requires mature quantum networks that don't exist yet, the researchers note it can be demonstrated on current quantum computing platforms, making this a bridge between today's capabilities and tomorrow's quantum internet security needs.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

In a shared quantum computer, how to ensure data privacy and protection from access by unauthorized parties? We propose a genuine quantum protocol for protecting user's data which is not accessible even to the service provider. The protocol is based on quantum kicked top -- the dynamics of a spin system --operating at quantum resonance regime. This protocol ensures perfect recovery for authorized users while making intercepted states appear mixed to eavesdroppers, with built-in tampering detection. This protocol can also be used for secure communication between two parties in geographically different locations, and also for quantum key distribution. The effectiveness of this protocol is demonstrated by assuming a quantum computer with quantum memory and functioning quantum networks. In the absence of the latter, at present, the protocol can be demonstrated in laboratory using currently available quantum computing platforms.
