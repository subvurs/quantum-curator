Entanglement reveals the difficulty of computational problems

Source: Physics World - Quantum February 10, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article explores a fascinating connection between quantum entanglement and computational complexity theory, suggesting that the amount of entanglement present in a quantum system can serve as a diagnostic tool for determining how difficult a computational problem will be to solve. The research builds on growing evidence that entanglement isn't just a curious quantum phenomenon, but a fundamental resource that governs the boundary between problems that are efficiently solvable and those that remain computationally intractable. This work could have significant implications for algorithm design and quantum advantage demonstrations, as it provides researchers with a new lens for understanding when quantum computers will outperform classical ones. The findings also deepen our theoretical understanding of how quantum mechanics fundamentally changes the landscape of what's computable, potentially guiding the development of more efficient quantum algorithms.

— Mark Eatherly
