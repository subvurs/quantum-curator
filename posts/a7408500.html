Multipartite controlled-NOT gates using molecules and Rydberg atoms

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 31, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This research demonstrates a clever hybrid approach that combines the complementary strengths of two promising quantum platforms: polar molecules provide stable, long-lived qubits while Rydberg atoms contribute their exceptionally strong interactions for rapid gate operations. The ability to implement multipartite CNOT gates, where multiple qubits can simultaneously control or be controlled by others, represents a significant step toward more efficient quantum circuits that could reduce the overhead typically required for complex quantum algorithms. Achieving over 99% fidelity for four-qubit operations while maintaining robustness against decoherence suggests this hybrid architecture could offer practical advantages for near-term quantum computing applications. The scalable nature of this approach particularly stands out, as most quantum platforms struggle to maintain high performance as system size increases.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

We propose high-fidelity controlled-NOT (CNOT) gates in a hybrid system of polar molecules and Rydberg atoms based on the unconventional Rydberg pumping mechanism. By combining the rich internal structure of polar molecules with the strong dipole-dipole interactions of Rydberg atoms, we realize both two-to-one and one-to-two gate configurations. Numerical simulations show that the gate performance is robust against spontaneous emission from Rydberg states. The approach naturally extends to larger systems, as demonstrated by four-qubit implementations achieving three-to-one and one-to-three CNOT gates with fidelities exceeding 99\%. These results highlight hybrid molecule-Rydberg atom architectures as a promising platform for scalable quantum information processing.
