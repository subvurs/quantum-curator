Photonic qubit encoding interconversion for heterogeneous quantum networking

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics April 02, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article tackles one of quantum networking's most pressing practical challenges: how to connect different quantum systems that "speak" different photonic languages. While some quantum devices encode information in photon polarization and others use time-bin encoding, this research demonstrates a clever interconversion protocol that acts like a universal translator, allowing polarization-encoded qubits to survive transmission through fiber optic cables by temporarily converting them to the more robust time-bin format. The team's approach of converting fidelity losses into transmission rate changes is particularly elegant, maintaining the quality of quantum entanglement even when the fiber introduces polarization fluctuations. This kind of encoding flexibility will be essential for building large-scale quantum networks where different institutions and companies need to connect their disparate quantum hardware platforms seamlessly.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Quantum information processing, communication, and sensing networks are being developed with various qubit platforms that use different encoding schemes. Connecting quantum network nodes to distribute entanglement requires matching photon qubit basis encoding. In this work, we implement an interconversion protocol which converts photon qubit encoding from the polarization basis to the time-bin basis, transmits the photons through a transport fiber with large fluctuations in polarization, and converts back to polarization encoding for ease of measurement. This interconversion scheme faithfully transmits a polarization Bell state across the transport fiber by converting sources of infidelity to changes in transmission rate. These results illustrate a practical approach for interfacing distinct qubit platforms to enable modular and flexible operation in heterogeneous quantum networks.
