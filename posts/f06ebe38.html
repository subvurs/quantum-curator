Emergence of volume-law scaling for entanglement negativity from the Hawking radiation of analogue black holes

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics April 02, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This research provides the first concrete demonstration that Hawking radiation creates a fundamentally different type of quantum entanglement than previously observed, with implications that extend far beyond theoretical physics into practical quantum simulation. The team shows that entanglement negativity - a key measure of quantum correlations - develops a volume-law scaling rather than the typical logarithmic behavior, suggesting that Hawking radiation creates long-range entangled pairs throughout the black hole's interior and exterior regions. What makes this particularly exciting is that these predictions could be tested in current cold-atom experiments, offering a pathway to probe black hole physics in the laboratory and potentially resolve fundamental questions about information loss during black hole evaporation. This work bridges quantum information theory, black hole physics, and experimental quantum simulation in a way that could illuminate one of the most profound puzzles in modern physics.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

The quantum information content of Hawking radiation holds the key to understanding black-hole evaporation and the fate of unitarity. Motivated by recent advances in cold-atom experiments, we develop a lattice-regularization approach aimed at simulating the coarse-grained entanglement scaling of a quantum field in a 1+1D analogue black-hole background. We provide the first concrete demonstration that logarithmic negativity -- an entanglement monotone that typically exhibits a UV-divergent log-scaling for the conformal vacuum -- acquires a UV-finite volume term from the nonlocal correlations seeded by Hawking radiation. We show that this volume term encodes both the number density and spatial distribution of entangled Hawking pairs along the black-hole interior and exterior. We highlight its prospective detection in currently realizable experiments as well as its implications beyond the analogue paradigm, in particular for black-hole thermodynamics.
