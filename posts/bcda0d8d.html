Quantum Computing and Error Mitigation with Deep Learning for Frenkel Excitons

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 25, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article demonstrates a promising convergence of quantum computing with condensed matter physics by tackling Frenkel excitons—quantum mechanical excitations crucial for understanding light-matter interactions in organic semiconductors and photovoltaic materials. The researchers' development of a deep learning framework for error mitigation represents a significant advancement beyond traditional post-selection methods, addressing one of the most pressing challenges in NISQ-era quantum computing. What makes this work particularly noteworthy is its validation on real quantum hardware rather than just simulators, suggesting that hybrid classical-quantum approaches using machine learning could be key to extracting meaningful physics from today's noisy quantum processors. The successful simulation of these optical excitations opens new pathways for using quantum computers to design better solar cells, LEDs, and other optoelectronic devices.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Quantum computers, currently in the noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era, have started to provide scientists with a novel tool to explore quantum physics and chemistry. While several electronic systems have been extensively studied, Frenkel excitons, as prototypical optical excitations, remain among the less-explored applications. Here, we first use variational quantum deflation to calculate the eigenstates of the Frenkel Hamiltonian and evaluate the observables based on the oscillator strength for each eigenstate. Furthermore, using NISQ quantum computers requires performing error mitigation techniques alongside simulations. To deal with noisy qubits, we developed a deep-learning-based framework combined with a post-selection technique to learn the noise pattern and mitigate the error. Our mitigation methods work well and outperform the conventional post-selection and remain valid on real hardware.
