Ab Initio Study of Erbium Point Defects in 4H-SiC for Quantum Devices

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 16, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This computational study tackles a crucial bottleneck in quantum technology development: finding defect centers that can be reliably manufactured using existing semiconductor processes. By using first-principles calculations to map out how erbium atoms behave when embedded in silicon carbide crystal structures, researchers are essentially creating a theoretical blueprint for quantum devices that could leverage the same mature fabrication techniques used in classical electronics. The focus on erbium defects in 4H-SiC is particularly strategic, as these systems can emit single photons at telecommunications wavelengths—making them ideal candidates for quantum networks that need to transmit quantum information over existing fiber optic infrastructure. This type of foundational theoretical work is essential for bridging the gap between laboratory quantum experiments and the scalable manufacturing needed for practical quantum technologies.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Identifying scalable materials systems that exhibit quantum behavior is a central challenge in quantum information science. Point defects in certain wide-bandgap semiconductors are promising in this regard due to the maturity of semiconductor manufacturing and ion implantation technology. Single erbium defect centers in 4H-SiC are examples of such defects that provide access to discrete defect-induced electron energy levels within the bulk material bandgap, which can be utilized for a variety of quantum technologies, such as single-photon emission for secure communication and distributed quantum computing. This work presents a first-principles study of erbium point defects in 4H-SiC using density functional theory. These results provide materials-level support for the development of Er point defects in 4H-SiC as a scalable platform for quantum devices, helping to bridge the gap between quantum physics and the practical realization of quantum networks.
