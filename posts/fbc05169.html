Microscopic Side Information Controls Ordered Hayden--Preskill Recovery

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics July 17, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

AI Commentary

This article shows that even a modest amount of classical “side‑information” – just the order or block location of received qubits – can shift the scaling law for Hayden–Preskill recovery from linear in the system size to the sub‑linear n2⁄3 regime, highlighting how much decoding power is hidden in seemingly trivial metadata. By rigorously linking the recovery threshold to random‑subsequence coincidences, the work bridges recent advances in quantum chaos, scrambling dynamics and practical error‑correction strategies that must operate with imperfect labeling on real hardware. The result suggests that future quantum processors could tolerate looser qubit‑tracking requirements without sacrificing information‑retrieval performance, though the analysis still relies on idealized Haar‑random scramblers and may need adaptation for specific device architectures.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

In the Hayden--Preskill protocol, the decoder is usually assumed to know the microscopic identity of the collected output qubits. We study what happens when these labels are unavailable and only the relative order of the received qubits is preserved. The resulting order-preserving deletion channel maps an $n$-qubit scrambled register to a subsequence of length $\ell$. For a diary of fixed size $k$, we prove that the optimal entanglement fidelity converges to the no-output value $4^{-k}$ when $\ell=o(n^{2/3})$, uniformly over the scrambling unitary. For a Haar-random scrambling unitary, it converges to one when $\ell=ω(n^{2/3})$ and $\ell=o(n)$. Monotonicity then gives the fixed-error recovery scale $\ell_{\mathrm{rec}}=Θ(n^{2/3})$. We also consider partial position information obtained by dividing the register into $B$ consecutive blocks and revealing the block of origin of each received qubit. The recovery scale becomes $\ell_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,B)\asymp n^{2/3}B^{-1/3}$ for $B\leq\sqrt n$ and $\ell_{\mathrm{rec}}(n,B)\asymp n/B$ for $B\geq\sqrt n$. The exponent $2/3$ is traced to rank-aligned coincidences between random subsequences, which control both the converse and the recovery argument. Thus, even purely classical information about the origin of the received subsystems can change the amount of quantum output required for Hayden--Preskill recovery.
