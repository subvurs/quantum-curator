Boosted linear-optical measurements on single-rail qubits with unentangled ancillas

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 17, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This research tackles a fundamental challenge in photonic quantum computing: how to perform arbitrary measurements on single photons when you can't easily make them interact with each other. The team demonstrates a clever workaround using unentangled ancilla photons and an 8-port interferometer to boost measurement success rates on single-rail qubits (where information is encoded in vacuum versus single-photon states) to nearly 60%, significantly improving on the previous 50% theoretical limit. This advance could be particularly impactful for quantum sensing applications and distributed quantum networks, where photons naturally serve as information carriers but their weak interactions have historically limited measurement capabilities. The approach represents an important step toward making photonic quantum systems more practical by squeezing better performance out of linear optical components.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Any quantum state of the radiation field, sliced in small non-overlapping space-time bins is a collection of single-rail qubits, each spanning the vacuum and single-photon Fock state of a mode. Quantum logic on these qubits would enable arbitrary measurements on information-bearing light, but is hard due to the lack of strong nonlinearities. With unentangled ancilla single-rail qubits, an $8$-port interferometer and photon detection, we show any single-rail qubit measurement in the $XY$ Bloch plane is realizable with success probability $147/256$, which beats the prior-known $1/2$ limit.
