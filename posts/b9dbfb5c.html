Circuit-Level Noise Estimation via Shuttling in Plaquette Circuits

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics June 03, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article tackles a critical challenge in scaling quantum error correction: how to estimate noise levels in quantum circuits when you can only take single measurements and don't have full control over the hardware calibration. The researchers cleverly use surface code plaquette experiments with two different qubit management strategies to extract noise information from limited data, which is especially valuable for cloud-based quantum computing where users lack low-level hardware access. By testing their approach on both ion-trap and superconducting systems, they demonstrate a practical method for characterizing error correction performance that could help researchers optimize QEC protocols even with restricted hardware access. This work addresses a real bottleneck in quantum error correction development, where understanding your noise landscape is essential but often requires privileged access to quantum hardware that most users simply don't have.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

We present a method for estimating QEC circuit-level noise levels assuming that only single-shot measurements are available (e.g. measurements are slow and performed in a zoned/parallel fashion), and that lower level quantum hardware calibration is not possible (e.g. cloud access) or not feasible (e.g. large scale computing). We develop and run surface code plaquette experiments using two syndrome qubit configurations: FRESH, involving fresh qubits for each plaquette repetition, and RECYCLE, reusing qubits. To validate our approach, we compile plaquettes to ion-trap (IonQ Aria1) native gate set and apply hardware-aware rewrite templates to reduce circuit depth and execution time. We also run the experiments on a non-shuttling, superconducting processor (IBM Torino). We estimate circuit-level noise rates from the resulting single-shot plaquette measurement statistics, and conclude numerically about the viability of low-depth QEC experiments.
