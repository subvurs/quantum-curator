Adaptive Negativity Estimation via Collective Measurements

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 26, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This research tackles one of quantum computing's fundamental challenges: efficiently measuring how entangled quantum states actually are, which is crucial for verifying quantum advantage and debugging quantum algorithms. The clever combination of collective measurements with LSTM neural networks represents a significant step forward in quantum state characterization, allowing researchers to adaptively optimize their measurement strategy in real-time rather than using fixed protocols. What makes this particularly exciting is the demonstrated improvement over non-adaptive approaches, suggesting that machine learning can genuinely enhance our ability to probe quantum systems with limited experimental resources. This adaptive framework could prove invaluable for quantum error correction and state preparation verification in near-term quantum devices where measurement overhead is a critical bottleneck.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

This paper explores an efficient method for entanglement quantification in two-qubit and qubit-qutrit quantum systems based upon the framework of collective measurements in conjunction with machine learning. We introduce an adaptive measurement procedure in which measurement settings are dynamically adjusted based on prior measurement outcomes aiming to optimize the inference precision given a limited number of these measurement settings. The procedure makes use of the Long Short-Term Memory networks to recurrently process collective measurements on two copies of the investigated states. Obtained results demonstrate the tangible benefits of the adaptive measurements in comparison to previously described non-adaptive strategies.
