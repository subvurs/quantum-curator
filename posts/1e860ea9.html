The 27-qubit Counterexample to the LU-LC Conjecture is Minimal

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 26, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article resolves a fundamental question in quantum graph state theory by proving that 27 qubits is indeed the smallest system where local unitary equivalence and local Clifford equivalence diverge - a distinction that has profound implications for understanding quantum state transformations and error correction. The result settles nearly two decades of uncertainty since the original counterexample was discovered in 2007, providing crucial theoretical grounding for quantum algorithm designers who need to understand which graph state transformations are possible using different types of operations. By connecting this problem to classical coding theory through triorthogonal and Reed-Muller codes, the work demonstrates how quantum information theory continues to bridge with established mathematical frameworks. This precision in understanding equivalence classes becomes increasingly important as quantum computers scale toward the fault-tolerant regime where efficient state preparation and manipulation are critical.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

It was once conjectured that two graph states are local unitary (LU) equivalent if and only if they are local Clifford (LC) equivalent. This so-called LU-LC conjecture was disproved in 2007, as a pair of 27-qubit graph states that are LU-equivalent, but not LC-equivalent, was discovered. We prove that this counterexample to the LU-LC conjecture is minimal. In other words, for graph states on up to 26 qubits, the notions of LU-equivalence and LC-equivalence coincide. This result is obtained by studying the structure of 2-local complementation, a special case of the recently introduced r-local complementation, and a generalization of the well-known local complementation. We make use of a connection with triorthogonal codes and Reed-Muller codes.
