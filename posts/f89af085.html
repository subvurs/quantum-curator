Fun with Graph States: Nonlocal Bell Pairs and the Arf Invariant

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics June 04, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This research unveils a surprisingly elegant mathematical structure underlying graph states, revealing that these widely-used quantum states can be decomposed into collections of nonlocal Bell pairs paired with unentangled ancillary qubits. The discovery that graph state properties are governed by fundamental algebraic concepts like the Arf invariant and adjacency matrix rank over finite fields provides new theoretical tools for understanding why measurement-based quantum computation works so effectively. This mathematical insight could lead to more efficient ways to prepare and manipulate graph states in quantum hardware, potentially streamlining protocols for quantum sensing and distributed quantum computing where graph states serve as essential resources. The work bridges quantum information theory with algebraic topology in a way that may unlock new perspectives on quantum advantage and inspire novel quantum algorithms.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

We study inner products and partial amplitudes of graph states--a commonly employed class of quantum states, which are specified by graphs. We find that the magnitudes of these quantities are simply related to the rank of the adjacency matrix of the graph over F_2 while the phase is determined by the Arf invariant of its quadratic refinement. These facts motivate a nonlocal tensor factorization of the Hilbert space, with respect to which all graph states are products of Bell pairs with unentangled ancillae. These results may illuminate the quantum advantage in the framework of Measurement-Based Quantum Computation and suggest that graph states can be usefully visualized in the language of algebraic topology. In addition, we develop a specialized technique for computing expectation values of qubit-wise permutations in graph states, which is useful for calculating multi-invariants.
