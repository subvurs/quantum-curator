Mitigating Dynamic Crosstalk with Optimal Control

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 25, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article tackles one of the most insidious problems in quantum hardware: dynamic crosstalk, where the very pulses used to control qubits accidentally affect neighboring qubits in unpredictable ways. The researchers' breakthrough lies in using the "perfect entangler spectrum" as a diagnostic tool to identify unwanted interactions, then designing optimal control pulses that eliminate these interactions with surprisingly minimal modifications to existing pulse shapes. This approach is particularly valuable because dynamic crosstalk has been notoriously difficult to detect and suppress compared to static forms of interference, yet it poses a major obstacle to scaling up quantum processors with many qubits. The generalizability of their control principle could significantly improve gate fidelities across different quantum hardware platforms, bringing us closer to fault-tolerant quantum computing.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

The prevalence of quantum crosstalk is an important barrier to scaling frequency-addressable qubit architectures, with dynamic crosstalk being particularly difficult to detect and suppress. This form of crosstalk refers to unintended interactions driven by the gate control fields themselves. Here, we minimize dynamic crosstalk using quantum optimal control based on the perfect entangler spectrum, where spectral peaks signal unwanted entanglement with spectator qubits. Focusing on parametric gates in tunable coupler systems, we derive pulse shapes that eliminate dynamic crosstalk. Remarkably, only minimal pulse modifications are required to mitigate the form of crosstalk that is otherwise most difficult to predict. The ability to suppress dynamic crosstalk via the perfect entangler spectrum establishes a generalizable control principle for eliminating unwanted interactions in quantum hardware.
