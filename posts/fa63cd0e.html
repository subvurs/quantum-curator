Useful quantum computers could be built with as few as 10,000 qubits, team finds

Source: Phys.org Quantum April 01, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This research represents a potential game-changer for the quantum computing timeline, as reducing the qubit requirements by two orders of magnitude could dramatically accelerate when we see practical quantum computers. The collaboration between Caltech theorists and the startup Oratomic suggests they've found innovative error correction techniques that work more efficiently than previously thought possible, addressing one of quantum computing's biggest hurdles. While current quantum computers from IBM, Google, and others are approaching the 1,000-qubit range, this finding indicates that truly useful quantum advantage might be achievable with systems containing 10,000-20,000 qubits rather than the millions once thought necessary. If validated, this could push practical quantum computing applications from the 2030s into the late 2020s, making quantum breakthroughs in drug discovery, cryptography, and optimization much more imminent.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Quantum computers of the future may be closer to reality thanks to new research from Caltech and Oratomic, a Caltech-linked start-up company. Theorists and experimentalists teamed up to develop a new approach for reducing the errors that riddle today's rudimentary quantum computers. Whereas these machines were previously thought to require millions of qubits to work properly (qubits being the quantum equivalent to 1's and 0's in classical computers), the new results indicate that a fully realized quantum computer could be built with as few as 10,000 to 20,000 qubits. The need for fewer qubits means that quantum computers could, in theory, be operational by the end of the decade.
