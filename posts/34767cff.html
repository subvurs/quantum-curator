Sensitivity of polaron-molecule observables to MDR/GUP-like ultraviolet deformations at low energies via quantum computing

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics June 12, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article presents a fascinating intersection of quantum gravity theory and practical quantum computing, using IBM's quantum hardware to explore how tiny modifications to fundamental physics might be detectable in many-body quantum systems. The researchers cleverly exploit the polaron-molecule crossover region where small ultraviolet deformations get amplified, potentially offering a new experimental window into quantum gravity effects that are normally completely inaccessible at low energies. What makes this particularly exciting is the demonstration on actual quantum hardware (the QRed superconducting processor), showing that near-term quantum computers might serve as sensitive probes for fundamental physics beyond the Standard Model. This work opens up the intriguing possibility that quantum simulators could become tools for testing theories of quantum gravity, bridging the gap between high-energy physics and accessible laboratory experiments.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

We show that impurity many-body observables can display enhanced sensitivity to ultraviolet deformations of generalized-uncertainty-principle and modified-dispersion-relation type at accessible energy scales. Using a deformed polaron-molecule Hamiltonian constructed to preserve the infrared sector, we quantify the impact of such deformations on spectral and Ramsey observables and implement the corresponding dynamics in a controlled quantum computing setting. We identify regimes near the polaron-molecule crossover where small ultraviolet deformations are strongly amplified, leading to experimentally resolvable changes in quasiparticle properties and spectral response. Our results establish a concrete sensitivity-based route to low-energy quantum-gravity phenomenology in a well-defined many-body platform and delimit the validity of the effective description. Furthermore, we report experimental validation on the QRed superconducting quantum processor (BSC-CNS).
