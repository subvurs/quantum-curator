Metasurfaces for neutral-atom trapping

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics May 28, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article highlights a potential game-changer for scaling neutral-atom quantum computers, which currently face significant engineering bottlenecks in creating the massive arrays needed for practical quantum applications. Optical metasurfaces offer a compelling solution by providing vastly superior pixel counts compared to traditional spatial light modulators, enabling researchers to create hundreds of thousands of optical traps for individual atoms rather than the hundreds typically achieved today. The ability to precisely control light at this scale could finally unlock the path toward utility-scale neutral-atom quantum processors, while also enabling more compact and integrated quantum systems. This represents a crucial intersection of photonics and quantum computing that could accelerate the timeline for building fault-tolerant quantum computers with the atom counts needed for real-world applications.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Trapped neutral atoms are one of the leading platforms for quantum information technologies, in particular for quantum computing, but scaling them to array sizes needed for utility-scale quantum computing is a major engineering challenge. Here we review optical metasurfaces as an enabling technology that provides fine control over the phase, amplitude, and polarization of light, with pixel counts far exceeding what is available with spatial light modulators (SLMs) and other active devices. The large pixel counts have recently led to demonstrations of arrays of optical tweezers with hundreds of thousands of sites and arrays of optical bottle-beams with complex three-dimensional trapping profiles. The flexibility and scalability of optical metasurfaces provides a route towards miniaturized, integrated, and highly scalable atomic experiments and instruments.
