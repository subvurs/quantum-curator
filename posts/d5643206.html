Matter may entangle with light far more easily near quantum critical points

Source: Phys.org Quantum June 01, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This research tackles one of quantum computing's biggest challenges: scaling entanglement from tiny laboratory systems to large-scale materials that could enable practical quantum technologies. The discovery that quantum critical points - special phase transitions where materials hover between different quantum states - dramatically enhance matter-light entanglement could unlock new pathways for quantum communication and sensing applications. By identifying conditions where macroscopic quantum effects naturally emerge, Si's work bridges the gap between fundamental quantum physics and engineering quantum devices that work at room temperature and larger scales. This finding could accelerate development of quantum networks that rely on strong matter-light interactions, potentially making quantum internet infrastructure more feasible.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Quantum entanglement is a state in which particles are entwined with each other. In this entwined state, the properties of one particle influence the other, even when they aren't physically close to each other. This phenomenon has often been observed in small quantum systems with only a few particles in them, where researchers can use it to store and process quantum information. Rice University professor Qimiao Si is interested in understanding and applying quantum entanglement to macroscopic systems with vast numbers of particles.
