Topological sum rule for geometric phases of quantum gates

Source: arXiv Quantum Physics March 31, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This article establishes a fascinating mathematical bridge between abstract topology and practical quantum gate operations, showing that the geometric phases accumulated during two-qubit gates are fundamentally linked to the topological properties of their underlying Hamiltonians. The key insight is that quantum gates from different topological classes must distribute these phases differently in measurable ways, providing a new theoretical framework for understanding and potentially optimizing quantum operations. Most intriguingly, the work proves that nontrivial topology is actually a necessary condition for generating entanglement, meaning only Hamiltonians with specific topological characteristics can create the quantum correlations essential for quantum computing. This connection between topology and entanglement could inform the design of more robust quantum gates and help explain why certain physical implementations are naturally better at maintaining quantum coherence.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

We establish a topological sum rule, $ν_U = \frac{1}{2π}\sum_nγ_n = 2mν_H$, connecting the geometric phases accumulated by a two-qubit system over a complete basis of initial states to the winding number $ν_H$ classifying its Hamiltonian. Implementations of the same gate from different topological classes must distribute these phases differently, making their distinction measurable through the Wootters concurrence. As a corollary, nontrivial topology is a necessary condition for entanglement: only Hamiltonians with access to $ν_H \neq 0$ can generate it.
