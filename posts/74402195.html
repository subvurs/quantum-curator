Silicon quantum computer performs logical operations for the first time

Source: Phys.org Quantum March 30, 2026 Curated by Mark Eatherly

Curator's Take

This breakthrough marks a crucial milestone for silicon-based quantum computing, demonstrating that the same material powering conventional electronics can successfully perform logical quantum operations with error correction. The achievement is particularly significant because silicon spin qubits offer exceptional coherence times while leveraging decades of semiconductor manufacturing expertise, potentially providing a more practical path to large-scale quantum computers than other qubit technologies. By proving that silicon can handle logical operations—where multiple physical qubits work together to create more reliable logical qubits—this work validates silicon as a serious contender in the race toward fault-tolerant quantum computing. The compatibility with existing chip fabrication infrastructure could dramatically accelerate the development timeline for commercially viable quantum processors.

— Mark Eatherly

Summary

Silicon is ubiquitous in modern electronics, and now it is becoming increasingly useful in quantum computing. In particular, silicon's compatibility with existing chip technology and its long coherence times in silicon-based spin qubits make it a promising material for scalable quantum computing. A new study, published in Nature Nanotechnology, has demonstrated silicon's use in a logical quantum processor, representing the first of its kind.
